Information and Media Literacy

In today's digital world, information is available at our fingertips through websites, social media platforms, messaging applications, blogs, videos, and online news sources. While access to information offers many benefits, it also presents challenges. Not all information found online is accurate, reliable, or trustworthy. False information, misleading content, and fake news can spread quickly, influencing opinions, creating confusion, and sometimes causing harm.

Information and media literacy is the ability to find, evaluate, understand, and responsibly use information from digital sources. It helps individuals make informed decisions, identify trustworthy information, and avoid spreading misinformation.

This module equips participants with practical skills to critically assess digital content, verify information before sharing it, and become responsible digital citizens.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, participants will be able to:

  • Understand the difference between reliable and unreliable information.
  • Identify misinformation, disinformation, and fake news.
  • Apply basic fact-checking techniques.
  • Evaluate the credibility of online sources.
  • Share digital content responsibly.
  • Use critical thinking skills when consuming online information.

Identifying Misinformation and Fake News

Misinformation

False or inaccurate information that is shared without the intention to deceive.

Example: A person forwards a message claiming a certain food cures a disease without verifying whether the information is true.

Disinformation

False information deliberately created and shared to mislead people.

Example: A fake social media post designed to influence public opinion or create panic.

Fake News

Fabricated or misleading information presented as legitimate news.

Common Characteristics of Fake News

  • Sensational or shocking headlines
  • Lack of credible sources
  • Emotional language designed to provoke fear or anger
  • Poor grammar or spelling
  • Manipulated images or videos
  • Claims that seem too good or too alarming to be true

Fact Checking Basics

Fact-checking is the process of verifying whether information is accurate before accepting it as true or sharing it with others.

Steps for Fact-Checking

1

Stop and Think

Avoid reacting immediately to information that triggers strong emotions. Ask: Does this seem believable? What evidence is provided?

2

Check the Source

Consider: Who published the information? Is the author identified? Is the organization reputable?

3

Verify Through Multiple Sources

Look for confirmation from established news organizations, government agencies, educational institutions, and professional organizations. If only one source reports the information, exercise caution.

4

Check Dates and Context

Sometimes old stories are reshared and presented as new events. Always check the publication date, location, and original context.

5

Examine Images and Videos

Images and videos can be edited or taken out of context. Ask: Does the image match the story? Has the image appeared elsewhere before?

STOP→CHECK→VERIFY→SHARE

Responsible Content Sharing

Every internet user plays a role in preventing the spread of false information.

Sharing Inaccurate Information Can:

  • Cause panic or confusion
  • Damage reputations
  • Spread harmful health advice
  • Promote fraud and scams
  • Create social conflict

Responsible Sharing Practices

Verify Before Sharing

Always confirm information from reliable sources.

Read Beyond the Headline

Headlines may be misleading — read the full article before forming conclusions.

Respect Privacy

Do not share personal information, private photographs, confidential documents, or sensitive community information.

Consider the Impact

Before sharing, ask: Is it true? Is it helpful? Is it respectful? Could it harm someone?

Give Credit to Original Sources

Respect intellectual property and acknowledge creators when using their content.

Critical Digital Thinking

Critical thinking is the ability to analyze information objectively and make reasoned judgments. In digital spaces, critical thinking helps individuals avoid manipulation and make informed decisions.

Key Critical Thinking Questions

Who Created This Content?

Consider the author, the organization, and their purpose.

Why Was It Created?

Possible purposes: informing, educating, entertaining, selling a product, or influencing opinions.

What Evidence Supports It?

Reliable information should include facts, data, research, and expert opinions.

Are Different Perspectives Considered?

Trustworthy information often presents balanced viewpoints.

Avoiding Confirmation Bias

People often believe information that supports their existing beliefs. Good digital citizens:

  • Remain open-minded
  • Consider alternative viewpoints
  • Seek evidence before accepting claims

Practical Activity

Information Detective Exercise

Information Detective

Work in groups to analyze examples of online posts, articles, or messages. For each example, answer:

  1. Who created the content?
  2. Is the source trustworthy?
  3. What evidence is provided?
  4. Can the information be verified elsewhere?
  5. Would you share it? Why or why not?

Groups present their findings and discuss their decisions.

Key Takeaways

Not everything online is accurate or trustworthy.

Misinformation and fake news can spread quickly.

Always verify information before sharing it.

Use reliable sources and compare information from multiple sources.

Think critically about the content you consume online.

Responsible sharing contributes to safer and more informed digital communities.
